Exploring Clocks in MET

What’s the last time you read time from a clock? For me, surrounded by digital devices such as laptops, smartphones, and smartwatches, clocks are no longer important to keep track of time. However, clocks have been playing a critical role since more than a thousand years ago in the form of shadow clocks, water clocks, mechanical clocks, etc.

And what does a clock look like in your memory? In my impression, besides the world’s famous clocks such as Big Ben, most of the clocks I have seen were designed with a simple clock face and hanging on the wall. While I was exploring clocks in the MET collections, I surprisedly found that clocks are not just limited in a similar design and a similar size. In this project, I will be visualizing the story behind each clock in the MET.

In the MET collection, among 500+ clocks exhibitors, 92 exhibitors include accurate measurements in three dimensions, height, width, and depth .
For this project, I will only focus on exhibitors that include three dimension measurements documented by the MET.

This isometric skeleton interprets the dimension of object 7421, Shelf Clock, into an isometric cuboid. With the cuboid, I will be analyzing the dimension of each object in three faces: front, side, and up .

In the example of object 7421, the height is 83.2 cm, the width is 29.2 cm, and the depth is 14 cm.

I did the same process for all 92 objects and generated the following graph from three perspectives.

I found out that the average width of all 92 objects is 37.5 cm, the average height is 108.8 cm, and the average depth is 21 cm.

In terms of the largest dimension, the tallest clock is 311 cm, the widest clock is 104 cm, and the thickest clock is 45 cm.
